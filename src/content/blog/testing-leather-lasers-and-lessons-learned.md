One of the things I want Virtual Ventures to include in the future is small handmade extras alongside the crochet work.

Not random bits thrown together for the sake of it, but useful little pieces that fit the style of the brand. Things like leather tags, wrist straps, keyrings, display pieces, labels, and maybe small engraved details that make the finished items feel a bit more special.

This week, I started testing leather with the laser.

Very carefully.

## Starting With Real Materials

I wanted to use proper leather rather than fake material that could be unpleasant or unsafe to cut. This is one of those areas where learning slowly matters.

It is tempting to grab any cheap material and start experimenting, but not everything belongs under a laser. Some materials can give off horrible fumes, and I would rather take my time than risk using something unsuitable just because it was cheaper or easier to find.

So this test was about getting used to the process with real leather and seeing how the laser behaved.

## Engraving the Logo

The plan was simple enough in my head: cut out a clean circle and engrave the Virtual Ventures logo inside it.

In reality, even simple tests teach you a lot.

Getting the circle right, lining the design up properly, choosing the settings, and deciding what should engrave and what should cut are all little steps that need practice. It is not just pressing a magic button and instantly getting a perfect finished piece.

I am learning how the machine thinks, how the material reacts, and how much power is too much.

## When the Test Teaches You Back

The first test did not come out perfectly.

The cut was too strong, and it left marks and residue where I did not want them. That was annoying, but also useful. It showed me that testing matters, especially before making anything that might become part of a product later.

I would rather make mistakes now, quietly, during the workshop stage, than rush something out before I properly understand the material.

That is part of why Virtual Ventures is still being built slowly.

The finished pieces matter, but so does the process behind them.

## Learning Before Selling

This is the kind of behind-the-scenes work that people might not always see.

Before there can be polished product photos, neat packaging, and tidy shop listings, there are tests. There are settings to adjust. There are scraps of material, notes, little failures, and the occasional moment where you stare at something and think, “Right, so we are not doing it that way again.”

That is not a bad thing.

That is how the workshop gets better.

I want anything sold through Virtual Ventures to feel thoughtful and properly made. Whether it is crochet, a printed planner, a laser-cut detail, or a small handmade accessory, I want it to have care behind it.

This leather test was not perfect, but it was a step forward.

And sometimes that is exactly what the workshop needs.
